Week 6:

3D Design & Printing

The assignment for week 6 was to design and slice a component for my final project. Also, we must use a photogrammetry application to scan something.

At first, I decided to model the engine of the plane I will build for my final project. I found a side view of the engine online and used the image as a canvas to create the general size and shape of the engine.


The result was great!


To create this engine, I first split the engine in half vertically, used the sketch feature and outlined the engine, then rotated the image around an axle.



With the engine shaft complete, I decided to make the turbines inside. This process required two sketches. Each sketch is a cross-sectional view of the fan. I then lofted the two sketches and used a "circular pattern" to create a turbine. Many of these turbines were created, all different shapes and sizes, to test which created the most thrust.


After these two items were modeled, I 3D printed both. I also had bought a brushless motor that can spin at 16650 RPMs. Adding all the pieces together with the circuit Arduino board, I created some sort of a working motor. Below is a video of the engine spinning, the code for my Arduino, and the wiring.




Because I had some extra time on my hands, I decided to model the rest of the plane. Little did I know it was going to be a ~30-hour journey. I imported some canvas I found from the official General Dynamics page for the f16, and I was off.

I first started with the vertical stabilizer. I wrap a layer around the engine and then easily traced the canvas to recreate the vertical stabilizer.


The next component I made was the horizontal stabilizers, and the horizontal stabilizer supports. These were a pain to model since I had to make sure all bodies were not intersecting. I also had to keep in mind the aerodynamics of the aircraft. The stabilizers were made so that the air pressure above the stabilizer would be lower than the air pressure below the stabilizer. Later on, I also did this with the wings.


I next created the middle section of the body. Doing all this was easy because I just made sketches and rotated them around 360 degrees. When the middle body was created, the plane started to come together.


I created the winds by first creating supports. These supports prevent the wing from bending at weird angles. After these supports were made, the wings were designed with the air pressure concept in mind.


Finally, I started designing the front of the plane. This part was difficult, and I still have not finished. I only completed the nose and bottom vent. Here are the results.

I decided to use 3DF Zephyr to scan a plane on the rug for the photogrammetry portion of the homework. I used the following photos:


The first time scanning the plane, I used default mode for everything. The process was relatively fast, but the mesh created was not great. Everything was blurring and in lousy resolution; it was almost like I was watching a 70's sitcom.


I decided to use high for everything the next time I used the photos. It took a long time to render everything, but the result was a lot better. I could actually make out the plane from the blur.


Through this photogrammetry experience, I learned that a) my phone has a terrible camera and b) higher resolution is definitely worth it.
