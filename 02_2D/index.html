Week 2:

2D Design and Cutting

The assignment for week 2 is to follow a tutorial and then assemble some components. I had some really basic knowledge of Fusion 360 but to to strengthen my understanding, I first watched these youtube videos:


These videos taught me how to make shapes other than rectangles and circles. I now realize the lack of unique shapes in all my previous Fusion designs. I messed around a little bit and ended up these masterpieces:


Now, it was time to model something. I first want to model something relatively easy so I can get the hang of Fusion 360. That's why I chose to model a key and doorknob. I used my caliper to measure the length of everything on the key, and then I made a sketch.


This process was surprisingly complicated. Because the key has angled sides, it was hard to measure certain lengths with the calipers. I had to use lengths I was able to measure to calculate other lengths. I incorporated construction lines to make a job a little easier. Even so, some side lengths were estimated. I also did not model the teeth and notches of the key because they were almost impossible to measure.

After finally finishing the sketch, I extruded it and used fillets to smoothen out all the edges.


I then started to make the doorknob. Making it was a lot easier than making the key because my doorknob is basically a cylinder with fillets. I started with a circle sketch, extruded it, and placed fillets on the edges of the circle.


To create the doorknob hole where the key will enter, I first sketched a rectangle that was the same as the cross-section of the key on the front side of the filleted cylinder. Then I extruded inward, cutting the inside of the cylinder. Finally, I created a triangle sketch on the wall of the cut and extruded it. The interior of the cylinder is now the same size as the key. I used the align feature and selected two planes and a snap point. The key and the doorknob fit perfectly with a sweet-looking animation.

After completing this first project, I felt confident in my skills in Fusion. I decided to take on a more challenging project: modeling my drone. As it turns out, modeling my drone was EXTREMELY difficult. There were so many rounded edges and curves. I decided to start with the battery.


I had to create three components: the body, the underbody, and the hat. Each component took me more than two hours because of all the little details that existed. For the body, I started with a standard rectangle and then customized it with connecting lines. I then trimmed the unnecessary lines and extruded them.


For the underbody, I created a sketch on the bottom of the body and then made the weird shapes necessary. After extruding these shapes, the general form of the battery started to become apparent.


By far the most challenging part to model of the battery was the hat. It was curved on the top and had so many angles. To start, I sketched a side view and then extruded it. Next, to fit the body to the hat, I cut out a piece of the hat so the body can fit nicely.


Below is how these three parts combine to create a battery:


By the time I finished the battery, I had realized there was no possible way to model the entire drone. So instead, I decided to model a quarter of the drone; this way, I could see how the battery fitted to the drone without modeling the entire thing.

I started with a base. The base included some calculations because there were so many angles.


I then had to create a sketch on the side of the base so that there could be angles on all three axes.


The next step was the most important. I had to hollow out the body I just created so that the battery could fit. To do this, I had to measure/calculate every length. Because the battery was such a weird shape, the process required more sketches.
